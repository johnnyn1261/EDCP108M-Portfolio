{Concepts}

First things first: What exactly is effective math learning?

Although seemingly trivial, it is important to understand what math learning is in order to know what study techniques you should change, keep and add. Simply put, math learning is the "acquisition of knowledge in mathematics." But in order to be considered effective, this acquisition of knowledge must be tied to three major concepts or domains: Affectiveness, Behavioral and Cognitive. Interestingly enough, these domains are also the learning outcomes of of EDCP108M. Knowing this, let's take a closer look at each one:

Affective

This aspect encompasses the physiological components involved in improving learning, such as self-perception, anxiety and understanding of strengths and weaknesses to name a few. For example, positive self-talk can increase learning effectiveness.

Behavioral

While the affectiveness concept focuses on the psychological aspects of learning, the behavioral concept has to do with the physical ones. From simply taking and reviewing notes to managing time and practicing, a lot can be physically done to improve one's math skills.

Cognitive

A mix of the affectiveness and behavioral concepts, the cognitive concept involves obtaining new ways of understanding material. This can be done by practicing critical thinking or trying different techniques to solve the same problem.


To read more about these domains of learning, feel free to visit this University of Dayton article. If you're like me, your perception of learning math prior to learning about these domains was limited to the cognitive domain. After all, solving problems with different techniques isn't exactly a new idea. On the other hand, the affective domain describes a number characteristics that never really occurred to me. Most notably, it was interesting to discover that "Internalizing values," and one's value system can heavily influence the learning process, especially in a social environment.

Keeping this new found realization in mind, I will be frequently refering to these concepts to emphasize how they work together.

Given the wide variety of helpful on-campus math resources, it is logical to take advantage of them and seek help when needed. Personally, I have always been guilty of trying to figure out everything on my own and thus wasting time digging through a textbook or scouring the internet. Saving time by going to office hours or attending a tutoring session is therefore certainly beneficial. On the other hand, a negative implication of these math-related campus resources is relying on them too much for help and understanding. A serious consequence of this is that one would not be able to learn by his or herself. As a result, these resources should never supplement the affective and behavioral aspects of learning, but rather work with them.
